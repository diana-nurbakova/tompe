# Theory of Mind as a framework for MT post-editing pedagogy

Translation has always been an act of mind-reading. When a translator renders a source text into a target language, they must simultaneously reconstruct what the author intended, anticipate what the target reader will infer, and calibrate their output to bridge the gap between these two mental worlds. **Theory of Mind (ToM) — the cognitive capacity to attribute mental states to others — provides a powerful unifying framework for understanding translation, evaluating machine translation output, and designing pedagogical tools that train students to think critically about MT errors.** This report synthesizes peer-reviewed research across cognitive science, translation studies, HCI, and educational psychology to build both a theoretical grounding and actionable design principles for a tool that trains translation students to critically evaluate and post-edit MT output through system-generated explanations and student-generated justifications.

The central argument is threefold. First, translation is inherently a multi-layered ToM operation that has been recognized but undertheorized in translation studies. Second, MT post-editing introduces a novel mentalizing layer — reverse-engineering a non-human agent's "interpretation" — that creates distinctive cognitive demands and vulnerability to bias. Third, the self-explanation and cognitive forcing literatures provide robust evidence that requiring students to articulate ToM reasoning (explaining *why* an MT error occurred) produces deeper learning than simply flagging errors, making ToM-informed explanation a powerful pedagogical mechanism.

## Translation as recursive mind-reading: the empirical evidence

The explicit connection between ToM and translation is surprisingly recent despite being conceptually obvious. **Sturm (2020)** produced the first book-length treatment in *Theory of Mind in Translation* (Frank & Timme), presenting three empirical studies using fMRI, eye-tracking, and keystroke logging to investigate how translation engages "the meta-representation of others." Sturm's core question — "does translation train this uniquely human capacity?" — reframes translation competence as partly a mentalizing skill rather than a purely linguistic one.

The most rigorous neuroscientific evidence comes from **Szpak, Alves, and Buchweitz (2020)**, who used fMRI to identify neural substrates of perspective-taking during translation. Participants processed short scenarios where critical answer pairs conveyed different communicative intentions across reading and translation tasks. The study found activation in the **left inferior frontal gyrus** — a region within the mentalizing network — during translation tasks requiring inference about communicative intent. This is direct neural evidence that translation recruits the same brain systems used for ToM.

The most recent and most explicitly ToM-focused paper is **Harrington Fernández (2025)**, who uses ToM as "a discourse-level theoretical framework in translation studies to explore how a translator's decision-making is influenced by shared cognitive processes." Analyzing the English translation of the Spanish children's novel *Manolito Gafotas*, Fernández demonstrates that the translator leveraged her ToM to "align with the publisher's belief about two idealised and homogenised target reader categories: morally conservative parents and morally immature children." He connects this to Dunbar's orders of intentionality, showing that literary translation can push toward fifth-order embedding — substantially beyond what most people handle in everyday interaction.

Despite this emerging work, **mainstream translation competence models do not explicitly incorporate ToM**. The PACTE group's influential model (Hurtado Albir, 2003, 2017) identifies five sub-competences — bilingual, extra-linguistic, knowledge-about-translation, instrumental, and strategic — but perspective-taking is only implicitly embedded within the strategic sub-competence that governs problem-solving and audience awareness. Similarly, Hubscher-Davidson's work on emotional intelligence in translation (2013, 2017) addresses affective ToM (empathy, emotion perception) but does not engage cognitive ToM theory directly. This represents a clear gap: translation studies has the empirical phenomena but lacks the explicit theoretical vocabulary that ToM provides.

## Relevance Theory already describes a ToM operation — without naming it

Ernst-August Gutt's application of Sperber and Wilson's Relevance Theory to translation (1991/2000) provides what is arguably the most complete theoretical account of how translation functions as a communicative act — and it is, at its core, a description of recursive mentalizing. Sperber and Wilson (1986/1995) define communication through two nested intentions: the **informative intention** (the intention to inform the audience of something) and the **communicative intention** (the intention to inform the audience of one's informative intention). This two-layer structure is itself a second-order mental state attribution: the communicator models the audience modeling the communicator's intent.

Gutt argues that translation is a form of **interpretive use** — the translator's utterance "represents some other utterance or thought, irrespective of the truth or state of affairs." Under this framework, the translator must perform at least four cognitive operations that map directly onto ToM:

1. **Reconstruct the author's informative and communicative intentions** — first-order ToM about the author: what did they mean to convey, and what did they intend the reader to recognize about that conveyance?
2. **Assess the mutual cognitive environment of the original communication** — modeling what was manifest to the original audience, which requires perspective-taking about a historically and culturally situated reader.
3. **Assess the cognitive environment of the target audience** — modeling what assumptions, knowledge, and expectations the target reader brings, a separate act of perspective-taking.
4. **Produce a target text achieving optimal relevance** — calibrating output so that the processing effort required by the target reader is justified by the cognitive effects achieved, which requires simultaneously holding both the author's intended effects and the reader's interpretive trajectory.

Gutt never uses the term "Theory of Mind," yet his entire framework presupposes it. As he writes, "the quality of a translation depends on how well it meets the psychological requirements of communication... what the translator can achieve depends largely on the context of his audience." Making this ToM dimension explicit strengthens the theoretical apparatus available to both translation studies and pedagogical design.

## Orders of intentionality: translation demands third-order mentalizing at minimum

Robin Dunbar's framework of orders of intentionality provides a precise vocabulary for describing the depth of mentalizing that translation requires. **First-order intentionality** is basic self-awareness ("I believe X"). **Second-order** is basic ToM ("I believe that you believe X") — the level typically tested in false-belief tasks and mastered by children around age 4–5 (Wimmer & Perner, 1983). **Third-order** involves communicative intent ("I intend that you recognize that I believe X"), and **fourth-order** is the minimum for literature "that goes beyond the merely narrative" (Dunbar, 2009). Normal human comfortable operating level is fifth-order; sixth-order represents the upper human limit.

Translation occupies a distinctive position in this hierarchy. At minimum, the translator operates at **third-order intentionality**: the translator (1) believes that (2) the author intended the reader to understand that (3) some propositional content holds. When the translator then produces a target text, the chain extends to fourth-order: the translator (1) intends that (2) the target reader will understand that (3) the author meant that (4) a particular content or effect was communicated. Literary translation — with its layered ironies, unreliable narrators, and implied readers — routinely pushes to fifth or even sixth order. Fernández (2025) illustrates this with Virginia Woolf's *Mrs. Dalloway*, which achieves fifth-order embedding of character mental states that the translator must parse and re-encode for a new audience.

**MT post-editing introduces an additional pseudo-intentional layer.** When a post-editor encounters a mistranslation, they must model what the MT system "interpreted" about the source. While the MT system lacks genuine intentionality, the post-editor adopts what Dennett (1989) calls the **intentional stance** — treating the system as if it had beliefs and goals to predict and explain its behavior. This creates a chain: the post-editor (1) infers that the MT system (2) "interpreted" the author's text as meaning (3) something different from what the author (4) intended the reader to understand. This is functionally a fourth-order operation, even though one of the agents in the chain is non-sentient. The cognitive load is real regardless of the ontological status of the MT system's "understanding."

No empirical study has yet measured translators' orders of intentionality during post-editing — this represents a significant research opportunity. However, the framework predicts that post-editing demands higher-order mentalizing than from-scratch translation in some respects, because the post-editor must juggle an additional agent's (the MT system's) "perspective" alongside author and reader perspectives.

## The illusion of understanding: mentalizing about machines that don't mentalize

When translators post-edit NMT output, they face a distinctive epistemic challenge: the output is often grammatically fluent, stylistically natural, and entirely wrong in meaning. **Martindale and Carpuat (2018)** demonstrated this empirically in their landmark study at AMTA, finding that users responded strongly to disfluent translations but were "surprisingly, much less concerned with adequacy." Fluency has an immediate effect on trust while adequacy problems go relatively unnoticed until the user independently discovers the error. **Koehn and Knowles (2017)** documented the same phenomenon from the system side: NMT "sometimes sacrifice[s] adequacy for fluency by proposing a translation that sounds plausible but does not convey the intended meaning."

This fluency trap is fundamentally a ToM problem. The fluent surface form functions as an implicit signal of competence — it suggests the system "understood" the source text — leading the post-editor to attribute more comprehension to the system than it possesses. This is **miscalibrated ToM about a non-human agent**, and it parallels findings in the broader human-AI interaction literature. Shanahan (2024) warned that "the more adept LLMs become at mimicking human language, the more vulnerable we become to anthropomorphism," while Bender et al. (2021) argued that language models are "haphazardly stitching together sequences of linguistic forms... without any reference to meaning" — the "stochastic parrot" critique. The post-editor who accepts a fluent but inaccurate NMT output has, in essence, committed a false-belief error in reverse: they attribute a belief (correct understanding of the source) to an agent that does not hold it.

Research on **mental models of AI** confirms that accurate understanding of system capabilities predicts collaboration quality. Gero et al. (2020, CHI Best Paper) identified three components of mental models of AI: global behavior, local behavior, and knowledge distribution. Crucially, they found that participants who better estimated the AI's abilities performed better in collaborative tasks. **Bansal et al. (2019)** showed that achieving human-AI team performance above either party alone depends on the human's mental model of the AI's **error boundary** — knowing *when* the system errs. This maps directly onto post-editing: translators who have accurate models of when and how NMT fails (e.g., with pragmatic nuance, rare terminology, long-distance dependencies) will be more effective post-editors than those who do not.

**Nourani et al. (2021)** found that the order in which users encounter AI strengths and weaknesses shapes their mental models through anchoring bias. Users who observed system strengths early were more prone to automation bias and made more errors, while users who encountered weaknesses first made fewer errors but underestimated the system. For MT post-editing, this suggests that **training sequences matter**: introducing students to NMT's characteristic failure modes before its impressive successes may produce more calibrated mental models and better post-editing performance.

The most actionable finding for pedagogical design comes from **Buçinca, Malaya, and Gajos (2021)**, who demonstrated that **cognitive forcing functions** — design interventions that compel deliberate reasoning — reduce overreliance on AI. Their three interventions (requiring a decision before seeing AI output, requiring explicit agreement/disagreement, and introducing wait times) all reduced automation bias. Grounded in Kahneman's dual-process theory, these interventions shift users from System 1 heuristic processing ("the MT looks fluent, so it's probably correct") to System 2 analytical engagement. A follow-up study (Buçinca, Swaroop, et al., 2025, CHI) found that **contrastive explanations designed with a model of likely human misconceptions** — essentially applying ToM *to the user* — significantly improved independent decision-making skills.

## A ToM-informed error typology: mapping MT errors to mentalizing demands

No existing literature directly maps MT error types to Theory of Mind demands, but converging evidence from error typology research, cognitive effort studies, and translation process research supports a novel theoretical framework that links error categories to specific perspective-taking operations. This mapping draws on the MQM framework (Lommel, Burchardt, & Uszkoreit, 2013), Temnikova's (2010) cognitive difficulty ranking, and Daems et al.'s (2017) comprehensive study of error types and post-editing effort.

**Accuracy and mistranslation errors require first-order ToM about the machine.** When the MT system produces a semantically incorrect rendering — for instance, translating "disclose a diagnosis" as "discover a diagnosis" (an example from Nitzke & Hansen-Schirra, 2021) — the post-editor must reconstruct what the MT system "thought" the source meant. This is structurally analogous to a false-belief task: the post-editor recognizes that the MT holds a representation of meaning that differs from the actual intended meaning. Temnikova (2010) ranks disambiguation errors high on cognitive difficulty precisely because they require inferring the intended meaning versus the system's selected alternative. Lacruz, Shreve, and Muñoz (2014) confirmed that mistranslations correlate strongly with cognitive demand as measured by pause-to-word ratios.

**Pragmatic and register errors require second-order ToM about the target reader.** When NMT produces a technically accurate but contextually inappropriate translation — using informal register where formal is expected, or failing to convey an implicature — the post-editor must model what the target reader will infer from the translation choice. This is reader-directed perspective-taking that goes beyond reconstructing the source meaning. Alos (2015) directly addresses pragmatic competence in translator training, noting that making implicit meaning explicit (or preserving its implicitness) requires sophisticated audience modeling. Vieira (2016) found that discourse-level concerns during post-editing did not correlate with standard cognitive effort measures, suggesting they represent a qualitatively different — and potentially underrecognized — type of processing.

**Omission errors require first-order ToM about the source author.** Detecting that the MT system has failed to render part of the source message requires the post-editor to hold the author's complete communicative intent in working memory and compare it against the MT output. Omissions are among the most prevalent NMT errors (Bentivogli et al., 2016) and rank moderately high on Temnikova's cognitive difficulty scale. They are especially insidious in NMT because the output remains fluent despite missing content — the absence is harder to notice than a misformation.

**Terminology errors require modeling the reader's domain knowledge expectations.** Fixing a terminological error requires understanding not just what the correct term is but why it matters to the intended audience — whether a cardiologist reading a clinical trial report or an engineer reading a technical manual. ISO 18587 lists domain competence as a required post-editor qualification precisely because terminological adequacy depends on audience knowledge. The MQM framework treats terminology as a separate top-level category because its evaluation is inherently audience-dependent.

**Coherence and discourse errors demand recursive multi-agent modeling.** Daems et al. (2017) found coherence errors among the most impactful on post-editing effort across all effort indicators. These errors require tracking information flow across sentences, maintaining the author's argumentative structure, and predicting how the reader will misinterpret the distorted version — a recursive perspective-taking operation involving all three agents (author, system, reader) simultaneously.

This hierarchy has a practical implication: **the shift from SMT to NMT has moved the center of gravity in post-editing from low-ToM errors (word order, morphology) to high-ToM errors (mistranslation, omission, pragmatic failure)**. NMT produces fewer surface errors but more meaning-level errors, and its fluency makes those errors harder to detect. Post-editing pedagogy must therefore explicitly develop the higher-order perspective-taking skills that NMT's error profile demands.

## Why explanation and justification work: the cognitive science of articulating ToM reasoning

The proposed pedagogical tool features two complementary mechanisms: system-generated explanations ("the MT likely misinterpreted X because Y") and student-generated justifications ("explain why you think this is wrong"). Both have robust empirical support, and both function as ToM exercises — but through different cognitive pathways.

**System-generated explanations perform ToM about the machine *for* the student**, modeling the MT system's probable "reasoning" and making it visible. This aligns with Hattie and Timperley's (2007) feedback framework, which identifies process-level feedback (explaining the reasoning behind errors) as more effective than task-level feedback (simply marking errors correct or incorrect). Narciss (2008, 2012) showed that elaborated feedback — providing explanations of *why* answers are wrong rather than just flagging them — produces significantly better learning in intelligent tutoring systems than knowledge-of-results feedback alone. For the MT context, an explanation like "the MT system likely interpreted the French word 'actuellement' through English cognate interference, producing 'actually' instead of 'currently'" provides a cognitive model of the system's error process that the student can internalize and apply to future instances.

However, **Bansal et al. (2021, CHI)** found that AI explanations did not increase complementary team performance and may actually increase the probability that humans accept incorrect AI recommendations. This is a critical design consideration: system-generated explanations must be structured to promote critical thinking rather than passive acceptance. Buçinca et al.'s cognitive forcing approach suggests requiring students to form their own judgment *before* seeing the system explanation.

**Student-generated justifications activate the self-explanation effect**, one of the most robust findings in educational psychology. Chi et al. (1989) established that students who spontaneously explained worked examples to themselves learned significantly more. Chi et al. (1994) demonstrated that prompting self-explanation after reading improved understanding even for declarative knowledge — high explainers achieved correct mental models while many unprompted students did not. Chi (2000) proposed two mechanisms: **inference generation** (filling in missing information) and **mental model repair** (detecting and correcting faulty understanding). Both are directly relevant to post-editing: generating a justification for why an MT output is wrong forces the student to infer what the correct interpretation should be and to repair their model of the text's meaning.

The meta-analytic evidence is strong. **Bisra et al. (2018)** synthesized 64 studies involving 6,000 participants and concluded that "having learners generate an explanation is often more effective than presenting them with an explanation." **Lombrozo (2006, 2016)** showed that engaging in explanation leads people to seek patterns and favor hypotheses supporting broad, simple generalizations — exactly the kind of learning transfer needed when students must generalize from specific MT error instances to general error patterns. Williams and Lombrozo (2010) found that prompting explanation promoted discovery of regularities through a "subsumptive constraints" mechanism: explaining guides learners to interpret material in terms of unifying patterns.

Within **Chi and Wylie's (2014) ICAP framework** — which ranks engagement modes as Interactive > Constructive > Active > Passive — self-explanation qualifies as a **constructive** activity because it generates outputs beyond what was provided. Receiving system explanations is active (engaging with provided material); generating one's own justification is constructive (producing new content). The ICAP framework predicts, and empirical evidence confirms, that constructive activities produce deeper learning than active ones.

The connection to **metacognition** is equally important. Kuhn (2000) explicitly linked ToM and metacognition as related aspects of meta-level cognition, proposing that both involve "the need to invoke a meta-level of cognition." Carruthers (2009) went further, arguing that mindreading (ToM) is *fundamental* to metacognition — we understand our own mental states by applying the same inferential processes we use to understand others' minds. Kloo et al. (2021) provided longitudinal evidence that early perspective-taking at age 2.5 predicted metacognitive insight 2.5 years later. When a student writes a justification for why an MT output is wrong, they simultaneously exercise ToM (modeling the author's intent, the system's error, the reader's likely inference) and metacognition (monitoring their own comprehension, calibrating their confidence, making their reasoning explicit). This dual activation is precisely what Schön (1983) described as **reflection-in-action**: the practitioner becoming "a researcher in the practice context."

## Design principles for a ToM-informed post-editing pedagogy tool

The converging research streams suggest eight evidence-based design principles for the proposed pedagogical tool:

**Principle 1: Cognitive forcing before explanation.** Following Buçinca et al. (2021), require students to form and commit to an initial judgment about each MT segment *before* revealing the system-generated explanation. This prevents the passive acceptance pattern that Bansal et al. (2021) documented and shifts engagement from System 1 to System 2 processing.

**Principle 2: Graduated intentionality scaffolding.** Organize error exercises along the ToM hierarchy — beginning with first-order tasks (identify what the MT "thought" the source meant), progressing to second-order tasks (predict what a target reader would infer from this translation), and culminating in recursive tasks (explain how the MT's misinterpretation would mislead the reader about the author's intent). This mirrors the developmental progression in ToM research and aligns with Temnikova's (2010) cognitive difficulty ranking.

**Principle 3: Explicit perspective labeling.** System-generated explanations should explicitly name which perspective is relevant: "Author's intent," "MT system interpretation," "Target reader inference." This makes the ToM structure visible and provides students with a vocabulary for perspective-switching, paralleling how Shulman's (1986) Pedagogical Content Knowledge requires teachers to make their understanding of student mental models explicit.

**Principle 4: Justification before correction.** Require students to explain *why* the MT output is wrong before allowing them to edit it. This leverages the self-explanation effect (Chi, 2000; Bisra et al., 2018) and the subsumptive constraints mechanism (Williams & Lombrozo, 2010), promoting pattern discovery and transfer rather than surface-level repair.

**Principle 5: Contrastive explanation design.** Following Buçinca et al. (2025), design system explanations contrastively — not just "the MT produced X" but "the MT produced X rather than Y because it likely processed the source as Z rather than W." Contrastive explanations that anticipate misconceptions improve independent decision-making skills significantly.

**Principle 6: Error-boundary calibration.** Following Bansal et al. (2019) and Nourani et al. (2021), include exercises that systematically expose students to both MT successes and failures to build accurate mental models of NMT error boundaries. Introduce failure modes early to counteract anchoring bias from NMT's impressive fluency.

**Principle 7: Process-level feedback on reasoning, not just on corrections.** Following Hattie and Timperley (2007) and Narciss (2008), provide feedback not only on whether the student's correction is right but on whether their *reasoning* about the error is sound. This targets the self-regulation level of feedback, which produces the deepest learning effects.

**Principle 8: Dialogic argumentation for complex errors.** For high-ToM errors (pragmatic failures, coherence problems), follow Kuhn and Crowell's (2011) finding that dialogic argumentation develops thinking skills. Allow peer discussion or system-student dialogue about ambiguous cases where the "correct" post-edit depends on audience modeling — these are the cases where Chi and Wylie's (2014) highest engagement level (interactive) applies.

## Conclusion: what this framework makes newly visible

Framing MT post-editing through Theory of Mind accomplishes something that existing competence models and error typologies do not: it reveals the *cognitive architecture* underlying post-editing skill and explains why certain errors are harder to detect than others in principled rather than ad hoc terms. The difficulty of catching a fluent mistranslation is not merely that it "looks right" — it is that the post-editor must perform a false-belief attribution about a non-human agent while simultaneously holding the author's communicative intent and the target reader's interpretive expectations. This is third- to fourth-order intentionality operating under the additional load of cross-linguistic processing.

The framework also explains why NMT's fluency improvement over SMT has paradoxically made post-editing harder in some respects. By eliminating the surface-level errors that served as cognitive cues (disfluent output signals "something is wrong here"), NMT has shifted the post-editor's task from primarily low-ToM correction to primarily high-ToM evaluation. Training must shift accordingly — from teaching students to fix obvious errors to teaching them to mentalize about machines, authors, and readers simultaneously.

Three questions remain genuinely open. First, whether translation expertise produces measurably enhanced ToM capacity (Sturm's 2020 question) or whether it merely deploys existing ToM capacity more effectively in a domain-specific context. Second, whether the intentional stance adopted toward MT systems during post-editing draws on the same neural mechanisms as genuine ToM about human agents — a question Szpak et al.'s (2020) neuroimaging paradigm could be extended to answer. Third, whether the ToM-informed error hierarchy proposed here (from first-order machine-modeling to recursive multi-agent reasoning) predicts post-editing performance better than existing effort models based purely on linguistic complexity. Answering these questions would transform a productive theoretical framework into an empirically validated model of post-editing cognition.
