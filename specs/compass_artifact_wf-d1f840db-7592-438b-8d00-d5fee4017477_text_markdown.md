# Literature grounding for MTPE-ToM design decisions

**Eight specific design choices in the MTPE-ToM tool — competence modeling, teacher-gated progression, layered explanations, human translation benchmarks, annotation scaffolding, cognitive forcing, MQM pedagogy, and metacognitive dashboards — each find substantial support in peer-reviewed literature spanning learning analytics, intelligent tutoring systems, cognitive psychology, and translation studies.** This report maps each design decision to its evidential base, identifying seminal works, key empirical findings, and the inferential chain connecting published research to the tool's architecture. The literature is robust across all eight areas, with particularly strong grounding for the cognitive forcing and scaffolding fading mechanisms.

---

## 1. Competence modeling rests on well-established ITS and translator training frameworks

The MTPE-ToM dashboard's skill model draws on two converging traditions: computational learner modeling from ITS research and domain-specific competence frameworks from translation studies.

**Knowledge component architecture.** Koedinger, Corbett, and Perfetti's (2012) Knowledge-Learning-Instruction framework, published in *Cognitive Science*, provides the foundational decomposition logic. KLI defines **knowledge components (KCs)** as acquired cognitive units inferred from task performance, classified into facts, skills, and principles — each requiring distinct instructional events. The framework maps learning processes (memory/fluency, induction/refinement, understanding/sense-making) to optimal instruction types, validated against **400,000+ hours of learning data** from the Pittsburgh Science of Learning Center. For MTPE-ToM, KLI justifies decomposing post-editing competence into discrete KCs (e.g., error detection, severity judgment, edit adequacy) with tailored practice sequences for each.

**Mastery tracking.** Corbett and Anderson's (1995) Bayesian Knowledge Tracing (BKT) in *User Modeling and User-Adapted Interaction* provides the computational mechanism. BKT maintains probabilistic mastery estimates per skill using four parameters — initial knowledge, learning rate, guess probability, and slip probability — updating via Bayes' theorem after each learner response. BKT remains the dominant approach in deployed Cognitive Tutors reaching hundreds of thousands of students. Its per-skill mastery probability directly maps to MTPE-ToM's need for tracking granular PE sub-competences.

**Making models visible.** Bull and Kay's Open Learner Model research (2007, *IJAIED*; 2010, Springer; Bull, 2020, *IEEE TLT*) establishes that externalizing learner models increases self-awareness, promotes reflection and self-assessment, helps students identify misconceptions, and supports navigation decisions about what to study next. The SMILI framework (2007) provides a systematic analysis space for comparing OLM designs. Bull (2020) documents that multiple-view OLMs — skill meters, bar charts, topic maps — accommodate diverse learner preferences.

**Stealth assessment.** Shute and Ventura's (2013) MIT Press volume on stealth assessment introduces methods for embedding assessment invisibly within learning activities using **Evidence-Centered Design** (competency model, evidence model, task model). Stealth assessment enables continuous, flow-preserving measurement — directly applicable to assessing error detection and quality judgment during natural PE activities without interrupting the translation workflow.

**Translation-specific competence.** The PACTE group's empirically validated model (2003, John Benjamins; 2009, *Across Languages and Cultures*; 2017, monograph) decomposes translation competence into five sub-competences: bilingual, extra-linguistic, knowledge about translation, instrumental, and strategic (the central governing competence). The EMT Competence Framework (European Commission, 2017) adds a practice-oriented structure with five areas — Language and Culture, Translation, Technology, Personal/Interpersonal, and Service Provision — used as accreditation criteria for EU Master's programmes. For MTPE-ToM, the instrumental sub-competence (PACTE) and technology competence (EMT) directly encompass MT literacy and post-editing skills.

## 2. Teacher-gated progression outperforms full automation

The design choice to implement teacher-validated (rather than fully automatic) level transitions finds strong support in the teacher-AI co-orchestration literature.

**The Lumilo evidence.** Holstein, McLaren, and Aleven's program of research provides the most direct support. Their AIED 2018 study demonstrated that **students learn significantly more when teachers and AI tutors work together** than under AI-only conditions. Lumilo, a mixed-reality system on Microsoft HoloLens, provides teachers with real-time analytics on student cognitive and metacognitive states while students work with an equation-solving ITS. Teachers using Lumilo spent less time on low-error students and more on students exhibiting unproductive persistence ("wheel-spinning"). In their *Journal of Learning Analytics* paper (2019), the co-design process revealed that teachers wanted not just awareness information but also **action recommendations paired with the ability to "second-guess" the AI's judgments** using concrete student error examples. Holstein and Aleven (2022, *AI Magazine*) formalized this as a "teacher-AI complementarity" design philosophy — the AI detects patterns and alerts, while teachers contribute contextual judgment.

**Dashboard-mediated teacher agency.** Knoop-van Campen and Molenaar (2020, *Frontline Learning Research*) found that when teachers integrated dashboard-prompted feedback throughout lessons (rather than in blocks), they delivered more effective feedback types. Their 2021 study in *Interactive Learning Environments* revealed an **equalizing effect**: dashboards helped teachers distribute feedback more evenly across ability levels, counteracting the natural tendency to focus disproportionately on struggling students. Van Leeuwen et al. (2014, *Computers & Education*) distinguished mirroring dashboards (descriptive data) from advising dashboards (recommendations), finding that teachers need control over action rather than automated decision-making. Xhakaj, Aleven, and McLaren (2017, EC-TEL) showed that ITS teacher dashboards improved teacher knowledge of student performance while keeping the teacher as the ultimate decision-maker on intervention timing.

A 2025 systematic review of 82 studies on AI-powered educational agents concluded that **"hybrid human–AI workflows, in which teachers curate and moderate LLM output, outperform fully autonomous tutors"** by combining scalable automation with pedagogical expertise. The optimal design for MTPE-ToM is thus a system that suggests level transitions while requiring teacher approval — preserving teacher professional agency while leveraging algorithmic pattern detection.

## 3. Layered explanations are justified by expertise reversal and cognitive load theory

The dual-layer explanation design — simplified by default, with optional technical depth — draws on three converging research streams.

**Expertise reversal effect.** Kalyuga, Ayres, Chandler, and Sweller's foundational 2003 paper in *Educational Psychologist* established that **instructional techniques highly effective for novices can lose effectiveness or actively harm learning for more experienced learners**. This reversal was documented across worked examples, split-attention, redundancy, and modality effects. Kalyuga's 2007 review in *Educational Psychology Review* extended this to longitudinal evidence, concluding that "whether information is redundant depends on the level of expertise of the learner." This directly justifies MTPE-ToM's default-simplified approach: detailed explanations that help beginning PE students become extraneous cognitive load for advanced learners.

**Progressive disclosure.** Nielsen's (2006) progressive disclosure principle — showing only essential options initially, revealing complexity on demand — maps directly to the layered explanation interface. Springer and Whittaker (2018) applied progressive disclosure specifically to explanation interfaces, finding that **transparency provided "on demand" removes confusions from unwanted explanations and adjusts to user requirements**. A 2025 study on progressive disclosure in AI clinical diagnosis systems confirmed that layered presentation from high-level summaries to detailed causal explanations helps users manage information load.

**Cognitive Load Theory (CLT).** Sweller, van Merriënboer, and Paas (1998, *Educational Psychology Review*) defined the three-load framework — intrinsic, extraneous, and germane — establishing that reducing extraneous load frees working memory for schema construction. Paas, Renkl, and Sweller (2003) explicitly connected CLT to the expertise reversal finding: as expertise grows, previously helpful support becomes extraneous load. Sweller (2010) refined this further with element interactivity theory. For MTPE-ToM, managing explanation depth via progressive disclosure directly operationalizes the CLT principle of minimizing extraneous load while preserving appropriate intrinsic complexity.

**Fading worked examples.** Renkl and Atkinson (2003, *Educational Psychologist*) established the theoretical framework for fading worked-out solution steps, showing it provides a smooth transition from guided study to independent problem-solving. Renkl, Atkinson, and Große (2004, *Instructional Science*) found that **learners acquired the most knowledge about principles at exactly the points where steps were faded**, as measured through think-aloud protocols. Atkinson, Renkl, and Merrill (2003, *Journal of Educational Psychology*) showed that backward fading combined with self-explanation prompts improved both near- and far-transfer performance. This supports MTPE-ToM's design of progressively shifting from fully explained annotations to learner-generated judgments.

## 4. Human translation benchmarks calibrate novice evaluators against a critical skills gap

The "human parity" debate in MT research provides the most compelling justification for including human translations alongside MT outputs in training.

**The parity claim and its dismantling.** Hassan et al. (2018, arXiv:1803.05567) claimed Microsoft's NMT system achieved "human parity" on WMT 2017 Chinese→English, defined as no statistically significant difference between MT and professional HT ratings. Toral, Castilho, Hu, and Way (2018, WMT) identified three critical flaws: the **translationese effect** (source texts translated from other languages inflated MT scores), non-expert raters with lower discrimination ability, and sentence-level evaluation obscuring document-level quality differences. When only original Chinese source text was used, parity disappeared. Läubli, Sennrich, and Volk (2018, EMNLP) showed that document-level evaluation revealed significant preference for HT, with MT preference dropping from **32% to 22%** when full context was provided. Läubli et al.'s collaborative 2020 paper in *JAIR* issued formal recommendations: use professional translator raters, evaluate whole documents, use original source texts, and avoid reference-based evaluation.

**Novice-expert discrimination gap.** Toral (2020, EAMT) confirmed that **non-translators evaluate MT more leniently than professional translators**, with lower inter-annotator agreement. This finding is the core problem MTPE-ToM addresses: without structured training, students cannot reliably distinguish fluent-seeming but error-prone NMT from high-quality HT. Including human translations as calibration anchors directly addresses this by giving learners an explicit quality reference point.

**MT literacy as trainable competence.** Bowker and Buitrago Ciro (2019, Emerald) first defined MT literacy components: understanding MT processing, use contexts, wider implications, and output quality evaluation. Ehrensberger-Dow, Delorme Benites, and Lehr (2023, *The Interpreter and Translator Trainer*) argued translators should become "MT literacy consultants." Mellinger (2017, same journal) identified significant skills gaps between what translation programmes teach and industry requirements, arguing MT content should be embedded across entire curricula. Moorkens (2018, *The Interpreter and Translator Trainer*) described a classroom evaluation exercise where students compared SMT and NMT output, finding their observations mirrored published research — demonstrating that **structured comparative evaluation is an effective pedagogical activity**.

## 5. Annotation scaffolding with progressive fading draws on robust CALL and worked-example evidence

The design of annotation overlays that progressively fade as learners improve combines evidence from L2 glossing research and scaffolding fading theory.

**Digital glossing effectiveness.** Yanguas (2009, *Language Learning & Technology*) found that dual-mode glosses (textual + pictorial) significantly outperformed single-mode and no-gloss conditions for both reading comprehension and vocabulary acquisition. Abraham's (2008) meta-analysis of 11 CALL glossing studies in *Computer Assisted Language Learning* found a **large effect size (ES = 1.40)** for incidental vocabulary learning from computer-mediated glosses, with substantial advantages over paper glosses. Yanagisawa, Webb, and Uchihara (2020, *Studies in Second Language Acquisition*) meta-analyzed broader glossing research, finding glossed reading yielded **45.3% immediate word learning** versus 26.6% for non-glossed reading. These findings establish that annotation overlays in digital environments produce robust learning gains — the basis for MTPE-ToM's annotation layer.

**Scaffolding fading.** McNeill, Lizotte, Krajcik, and Marx (2006, *Journal of the Learning Sciences*) demonstrated that faded scaffolds produced stronger student explanations than continuous scaffolds in science education. Gidalevich and Kramarski (2019, *Instructional Science*) found faded self-regulatory scaffolds outperformed fixed scaffolds, particularly for long-term retention and sense-making. However, Martin, Tissenbaum, Gnesdilow, and Puntambekar (2019, *Instructional Science*) cautioned that fading can occur **too quickly**, decreasing performance when support dropped from medium to low — emphasizing that teacher scaffolding must complement material scaffolds. Puntambekar and Hübscher (2005, *Educational Psychologist*) argued that fading is a necessary but often missing characteristic of scaffolding, calling for adaptive rather than static removal of support. Belland, Walker, and Kim's (2017) meta-analysis of 333 scaffolding outcomes in *Review of Educational Research* confirmed that only **16.5% of scaffolding studies included fading** — a gap MTPE-ToM addresses by design.

**Translation-specific gap.** No peer-reviewed studies were found applying worked-example fading specifically to translator training, making this a genuine literature gap that MTPE-ToM could fill. The closest analogues come from general cognitive load theory (Renkl et al.) and process-oriented translation pedagogy (Kiraly, 1995; Jääskeläinen, 2002).

## 6. Cognitive forcing before feedback reduces overreliance and deepens reasoning

The requirement to justify quality judgments before receiving feedback is supported by converging evidence from HCI, cognitive science, and medical education.

**Cognitive forcing functions.** Buçinca, Malaya, and Gajos (2021, *PACM HCI/CSCW*) tested three forcing interventions informed by dual-process theory with 199 participants. The "decide-first" condition — requiring users to commit to a judgment before seeing AI recommendations — **significantly reduced overreliance** compared to standard explainable AI approaches. A critical trade-off emerged: interventions that reduced overreliance the most received the **least favorable subjective ratings**, and benefits were moderated by Need for Cognition. Gajos and Mamykina (2022, IUI) found that concurrent AI advice leads to more superficial engagement and anchoring effects, further supporting the commit-before-reveal design.

**The self-explanation effect.** Chi, Bassok, Lewis, Reimann, and Glaser's (1989) seminal *Cognitive Science* paper discovered that the best learners spontaneously generated self-explanations while studying worked examples. Chi, de Leeuw, Chiu, and LaVancher (1994, *Cognitive Science*) demonstrated that prompted self-explanation produced greater learning gains — **all high explainers achieved the correct mental model** while many low explainers did not. Bisra, Liu, Nesbit, Salimi, and Winne's (2018) meta-analysis of 64 studies (~6,000 participants) in *Educational Psychology Review* confirmed that generating explanations is generally more effective than receiving them, across subject domains and knowledge types.

**Deliberate reflection in medical diagnosis.** Mamede, Schmidt, and Penaforte (2008, *Medical Education*) found that reflective reasoning improved diagnostic accuracy for **complex, unusual cases** among internal medicine residents. Mamede et al. (2010, *JAMA*) showed deliberate reflection helped residents overcome availability bias. The deliberate reflection procedure requires physicians to commit to an initial diagnosis, list supporting and contradicting findings, generate alternatives, and re-rank — a "commit-then-reflect" structure directly paralleling MTPE-ToM's justification-before-feedback mechanism. Mamede and Schmidt's (2023) comprehensive review in *Medical Education* confirmed deliberate reflection as **consistently one of the most effective cognitive interventions** for diagnostic accuracy across 15 years of research. Fernandes et al. (2021, *Medical Education*) added that cued reflection outperformed free reflection, suggesting MTPE-ToM should provide structured justification prompts rather than open-ended ones.

**Predict-Observe-Explain.** White and Gunstone's (1992) POE framework establishes the broader principle: requiring commitment to a prediction with justification before observing outcomes activates prior knowledge, creates expectation violations that are memorable, and motivates deeper explanation-seeking.

## 7. MQM works with novice raters and has been pedagogically adapted

Evidence supports using MQM-derived error typologies in student training, with existing simplified variants demonstrating feasibility.

**MQM foundations.** Lommel, Burchardt, and Uszkoreit (2013, Aslib/TC35) introduced MQM as an open, flexible quality assessment system. Lommel, Uszkoreit, and Burchardt (2014, *Revista Tradumàtica*) detailed the hierarchical typology: 100+ issue types organized under Accuracy, Fluency, Terminology, and Style, with three severity levels (minor = 1, major = 5, critical = 25). The "MQM Core" provides a reduced **12-dimension entry point**. Since WMT 2021, MQM has been the de facto standard for MT human evaluation.

**Viability with novice raters.** Mariana, Cox, and Melby (2015, *JoSTrans*) directly tested MQM with student evaluators. Nine novice and two expert raters assessed 29 student translations. Novice raters were **internally self-consistent** (reliable) but varied in severity. With Many-Facet Rasch Measurement, a fair average could be computed even when raters were not interchangeable. The ATA-to-MQM mapping (22 of 24 categories successfully mapped) was created by Koby, Lommel, and Melby. Average rating time was **12.1 minutes per translation** — practical for classroom use. Koby, Fields, Hague, Lommel, and Melby (2014, *Revista Tradumàtica*) established the theoretical grounding: translation quality requires "accuracy and fluency for the audience and purpose."

**MTPEAS: purpose-built pedagogical adaptation.** Bodart, Piette, and Lefer (2024, *Translation Spaces*) introduced the Machine Translation Post-Editing Annotation System with **7 categories in plain language**: value-adding edits, successful edits, unnecessary edits, incomplete edits, error-introducing edits, unsuccessful edits, and missing edits. Tags use NE (no error in MT segment) and E (error) prefixes. The system was piloted with 30 students' post-edited texts and is part of the POST-EDIT ME! project (Lefer, Bodart, Obrusník, & Piette, 2023, EAMT). MTPEAS represents the most directly relevant existing work — a pedagogically-oriented simplification of structured quality annotation for PE training, with a decision-tree annotation methodology and three-text comparison approach (source, raw MT, student post-edit).

**Comparative evaluation in curriculum.** Guerberof Arenas and Moorkens (2019, *JoSTrans*) described MT/PE courses at UAB and DCU Master's programmes using project-based learning with comparison of RBMT, SMT, and NMT outputs. Doherty, Moorkens, Gaspari, and Castilho (2018, Springer) addressed the broader need for education and training specifically in translation quality assessment. O'Brien (2002, EAMT) provided one of the earliest PE course content proposals.

## 8. Metacognitive dashboards need more than awareness — they need actionable design

The learning analytics dashboard literature reveals a consistent finding: awareness alone is insufficient. Effective metacognitive dashboards must provide reference frames, action support, and theoretical grounding.

**Foundational dashboard frameworks.** Verbert, Duval, Klerkx, Govaerts, and Santos (2013, *American Behavioral Scientist*) introduced the **awareness → reflection → sensemaking → impact** framework for LA dashboard analysis. Verbert et al. (2014, *Personal and Ubiquitous Computing*) extended this, identifying key design dimensions and noting that most evaluations fail to assess whether dashboards produce behavior change. Verbert, Ochoa, De Croon, Dourado, and De Laet (2020, LAK) reviewed the field's evolution and called for stronger theoretical underpinnings and responsible design.

**Awareness is not enough.** Jivet, Scheffel, Drachsler, and Specht (2017, EC-TEL) analyzed how dashboards integrate learning science theories and concluded that **very few take educational foundations into account**. Dashboards rarely support metacognition or offer information about effective learning strategies. Jivet et al. (2020, *Internet and Higher Education*) identified three factors supporting student sense-making: **transparency of design, reference frames, and support for action**. Students with higher self-regulated learning scores perceived all three as more relevant, and learner goals (mastery vs. passing) affected interpretation of the same dashboard data.

**SRL grounding is weak.** Bodily and Verbert (2017, *IEEE TLT*) reviewed 93 articles on student-facing LA systems, finding that only **15 evaluated actual behavioral effects** and 14 evaluated achievement effects. Matcha, Uzir, Gašević, and Pardo (2020, *IEEE TLT*) analyzed dashboards through Winne and Hadwin's (1998) SRL model and concluded that existing dashboards **"cannot be suggested to support metacognition"** based on current evidence — they do not offer information about effective learning tactics and strategies. They proposed the MULAS model (theory, design, feedback, evaluation) as a corrective.

**Metacognitive mechanisms.** Chen, Lu, Goda, Shimada, and Yamada (2020, CELDA) found that dashboard visual attraction and reflection behaviors (revising annotations) positively affected knowledge of cognition, while perceived behavioral change affected regulation of cognition. Bull and Kay (2008, ITS workshop) directly connected OLMs to metacognitive monitoring (assessing one's own knowledge) and control (planning what to study). Sedrakyan, Malmberg, Verbert, Järvelä, and Kirschner (2020, *Computers in Human Behaviour*) linked dashboard design to learning regulation but noted that dashboards rarely generate feedback automatically based on learning processes. Bodily, Kay, Aleven, Jivet, Davis, Xhakaj, and Verbert (2018, LAK) bridged the OLM and LAD literatures, finding convergence in purposes (making learning visible) but divergence in visualization approaches (skill meters vs. activity charts).

For MTPE-ToM, these findings dictate specific requirements: the competence dashboard must provide not only skill-level visualization but also **comparison reference frames** (peer benchmarks, expert standards), **actionable next-step recommendations**, and **explicit learning strategy guidance**. Without these elements, the research consistently predicts that awareness-only dashboards will fail to produce metacognitive or behavioral gains.

## Conclusion

The eight design decisions in MTPE-ToM each rest on substantial empirical foundations, though the strength of evidence varies. The **strongest grounding** exists for cognitive forcing functions (Buçinca et al. 2021 + Mamede et al.'s 15-year medical education program), layered explanations (Kalyuga et al.'s expertise reversal effect + Sweller's CLT), and annotation scaffolding fading (Renkl et al.'s worked examples + CALL glossing meta-analyses). The **most novel contribution** of MTPE-ToM lies in combining these mechanisms — no existing system integrates progressive annotation fading, commit-before-feedback, and dual explanation layers in a translation training context. The MQM-as-pedagogy evidence is emerging, with MTPEAS (Bodart et al. 2024) and Mariana, Cox, and Melby (2015) providing the critical proof-of-concept for novice raters. The metacognitive dashboard literature carries a productive warning: Jivet et al. (2017) and Matcha et al. (2020) demonstrate that awareness-only designs consistently fail, mandating that MTPE-ToM's dashboard include actionable guidance, reference frames, and SRL scaffolding. The teacher-gated progression design aligns with Holstein et al.'s finding that teacher-AI co-orchestration outperforms automation alone, while the human translation benchmark design draws its urgency from the parity debate's demonstration that untrained evaluators systematically over-rate NMT quality.
